Section 5 · Scenario D
Ground Lease the Land to a Developer
Keep the Land · Monthly Income · Tax Deferral
You keep title to the land. A developer leases it from you for 75–99 years, builds and finances an apartment building on it, and pays you ground rent every month. You never sell. Your heirs inherit Manhattan Beach land.

How a Ground Lease Works

The developer pays you two things: (1) Ground rent — an annual payment, typically 4.5%–6% of the land's value, paid monthly; and (2) Key money — a one-time upfront cash payment at signing in exchange for a lower rent in early years.

You retain ownership of the land. At the end of the lease term, the building and all improvements revert to your estate.

Risks and Considerations

  • Subordination. The developer's construction lender will likely require Evan to subordinate his land interest to their loan — meaning if the developer defaults, the lender could foreclose. This is negotiable with a skilled ground lease attorney (notice and cure rights, standstill agreements). Must be addressed.
  • Narrower buyer pool. Not every developer accepts a ground lease. Affordable housing developers and institutional operators are the most common ground lease tenants.
  • Complexity and timeline. 6–12 months to negotiate and execute (the lease itself runs 75–99 years). Requires a real estate attorney with ground lease experience. Legal costs: $25K–$50K.
  • Liquidity. If Evan needs all cash at once, the ground lease doesn't deliver that. It is a long-term income play.
Tax note (not tax advice — consult your CPA): An outright sale triggers capital gains on the appreciation above your allocated cost basis. Your total basis is $3.3M across all four parcels — the Sepulveda portion is roughly $2.0M–$2.2M depending on how your CPA allocates it. On a $4.75M developer sale, that's ~$2.55M–$2.75M of taxable gain, or roughly $750K–$1M in combined federal and state tax. A ground lease is not a sale — no gain is realized, no tax is owed. That alone could preserve $750K–$1M in capital.
